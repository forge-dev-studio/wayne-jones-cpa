Running a small business in Rome or anywhere across Northwest Georgia means wearing a lot of hats. You're the owner, the manager, the salesperson, and—for many business owners—the bookkeeper and tax preparer too. That works fine in the early days. But at some point, the financial side of your business outgrows what spreadsheets and software can handle on their own.

The question we hear often is: *When is the right time to hire a CPA?* Here's our honest answer.

---

## When DIY or Software Isn't Enough Anymore

Tax software has improved a lot over the years, and there's nothing wrong with using it when your situation is genuinely simple. But "simple" doesn't stay simple for long once a business starts growing.

You've probably outgrown the do-it-yourself approach if:

- You're spending more than a few hours a month just trying to stay on top of your books
- You're not confident your income and expenses are categorized correctly
- You aren't sure what you owe in quarterly estimated taxes—or whether you owe them at all
- You missed a deduction last year and aren't sure what else you might be leaving on the table
- Your business structure has changed or you're thinking about changing it
- You received a letter from the IRS or the Georgia Department of Revenue

Any one of these is worth a conversation with a CPA. Several of them together means you're overdue.

---

## Key Trigger Moments for Hiring a CPA

### Starting a Business

The decisions you make at the beginning—how you structure your business, how you set up your accounting, how you handle estimated taxes from day one—have a long tail. Getting set up correctly is much easier than correcting mistakes later. If you're launching a business in Floyd County or the surrounding area, a CPA can help you start on solid footing.

### Adding Employees and Running Payroll

Payroll is one of the most compliance-heavy areas of small business finance. When you bring on your first W-2 employee, you take on obligations for withholding, depositing, and reporting payroll taxes on a schedule the IRS takes seriously. Mistakes here are costly and can come back years later. This is a natural moment to bring a CPA into the picture.

### Real Growth—More Revenue, More Complexity

Growth is good news. But more revenue usually means more complexity: more vendors, more transactions, potentially multiple income streams or locations. At some point the volume alone makes professional oversight worth every dollar.

### An IRS Notice or State Tax Letter

If you've received a notice from the IRS or Georgia Department of Revenue, don't try to handle it alone. Even if the notice turns out to be minor, a CPA can review what's actually being asked, respond correctly, and make sure the issue is genuinely resolved rather than just delayed.

### Making a Major Entity or Ownership Decision

Thinking about converting from a sole proprietorship to an LLC or S-corp? Considering bringing on a business partner? Planning an ownership change or exit? These decisions have real tax implications that vary depending on your situation and current law. Getting guidance before you sign anything is far more valuable than trying to unwind a decision after the fact.

### Reclaiming Your Time and Peace of Mind

This one is underrated. If financial worry is keeping you up at night—or if you're spending hours on taxes and bookkeeping that could be spent running your business or with your family—that's reason enough. Time has value. So does sleeping well during tax season.

## CPA vs. Bookkeeper vs. DIY Software

These three serve different roles, and it's worth understanding the distinction.

**DIY tax software** works well for simple situations. It walks you through a process, but it doesn't know your business, flag things that look off, or advise you on strategy.

**A bookkeeper** handles the day-to-day recording of transactions—categorizing income and expenses, reconciling accounts, keeping your records organized. This is genuinely valuable work. But bookkeepers are generally not licensed to give tax advice, prepare business tax returns, or represent you before the IRS.

**A CPA** (Certified Public Accountant) is licensed, holds ongoing education requirements (confirm current requirements in your state), and is qualified to prepare and sign business tax returns, advise on tax strategy, represent clients before the IRS, and weigh in on larger financial decisions. For a growing business, you often benefit from having both a bookkeeper keeping things tidy throughout the year and a CPA overseeing the bigger picture.

---

## What a CPA Does Year-Round (Not Just April)

A common misconception is that a CPA's job is basically to file your taxes once a year. In practice, the most valuable work happens throughout the year.

A CPA working with a small business owner might:

- Review your books quarterly to catch errors and flag anything unusual
- Calculate and remind you about estimated tax payments (quarterly deadlines exist—confirm current-year dates)
- Advise on the tax impact of a major purchase or capital investment before you make it
- Help you understand whether your business structure is still serving you well
- Respond to IRS or state correspondence on your behalf
- Meet with you before year-end to plan—not just report

By the time tax season arrives, there should be no surprises. That's the goal.
