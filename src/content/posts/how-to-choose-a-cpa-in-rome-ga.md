Hiring an accountant is one of those decisions that affects your finances for years. Get it right and you have a trusted advisor who keeps you compliant, saves you money, and helps you plan ahead. Get it wrong and you may face missed deadlines, unexpected tax bills, or worse.

If you're searching for a CPA in Rome, GA — whether you're a small business owner, a W-2 employee with a complicated return, or someone who's just moved to the area — this guide walks you through exactly what to look for.

---

## 1. Confirm They're Actually a Licensed CPA

"Accountant" and "tax preparer" are not protected titles. Anyone can hang a shingle. A **Certified Public Accountant (CPA)**, however, must pass a rigorous exam, meet education and experience requirements, and maintain an active license with the state board.

Before you hire anyone in Georgia, verify their license:

- Visit **cpaverify.org** (the official national CPA license verification site) or the Georgia State Board of Accountancy's website.
- Search by name and confirm the license status is **active**.
- Note the state(s) of licensure — this matters if you have income or business activity in multiple states.

Don't skip this step. A few minutes of verification can save you from a costly relationship with someone who isn't qualified.

---

## 2. Look for Relevant Experience and Specialties

A CPA who has spent thirty years doing corporate audits may not be the best fit for a sole proprietor or a family with rental properties. Ask about their typical client profile:

- Do they work with individuals, small businesses, or both?
- What industries do they know well?
- How long have they been in practice?

General experience matters, but **local and industry-specific experience** matters more. A CPA who has worked in NW Georgia for decades understands the regional business environment, local tax issues, and the types of entities common in this area — from contractors and retailers to medical practices and agricultural operations.

---

## 3. Find Out Who Actually Does Your Work

Some firms take you on as a client and then hand your files to a junior staff member you've never met. Your initial meeting is with a partner, but your actual work is done by someone with two years of experience.

Ask directly: **"Will I work with you personally, or will my account be managed by someone else?"**

Working directly with the CPA who signed on to serve you has real advantages. They know your situation from the first conversation. There's no translation layer between you and the person making decisions about your return or your books.

---

## 4. Evaluate Local Knowledge and Accessibility

A CPA based in your community understands things an out-of-town firm simply doesn't. They know local business conditions, they're reachable by phone without going through layers of staff, and they often have relationships with local banks, attorneys, and other professionals who can refer you when you need help outside their scope.

For Rome and Floyd County residents and business owners specifically, ask whether the CPA is familiar with:

- Georgia-specific tax credits and incentives
- Alabama tax obligations if you have activity across the state line
- Local industry norms (NW Georgia has a distinctive mix of manufacturing, agriculture, healthcare, and small retail)

---

## 5. Understand the Full Range of Services

Many people hire a CPA only at tax time and leave significant value on the table the rest of the year. A good CPA can provide much more:

- **Bookkeeping and accounting** — keeping your records clean so there are no surprises at year-end
- **Payroll** — ensuring employees are paid correctly and payroll taxes are filed on time
- **Tax preparation and planning** — not just filing, but proactive strategies to minimize what you owe
- **Business advisory** — help with entity selection, financial decisions, and growth planning

Having these services under one roof with one trusted advisor — rather than cobbling together a bookkeeper, a payroll service, and a seasonal tax preparer — creates consistency and reduces the risk of things falling through the cracks.

---

## 6. Consider Multi-State Needs

If you live near the Georgia–Alabama border, or if your business operates across state lines, you need a CPA who is licensed and experienced in **both states**.

Multi-state tax situations are genuinely complex. Residency rules, sourcing rules for business income, and reciprocity agreements (or lack thereof) can all affect what you owe and where. A CPA licensed only in one state may not be equipped to handle this correctly.

---

## 7. Ask About Communication and Year-Round Availability

Some accountants are essentially seasonal. They process returns from January through April and are difficult to reach the rest of the year. If you run a business, make a major financial decision mid-year, or receive an IRS notice in August, that's a problem.

Ask prospective CPAs:

- How do you prefer clients to reach you — phone, email, portal?
- What's your typical response time?
- Are you available for questions outside of tax season?
- How do you handle IRS or state notices?

A good CPA is a year-round resource, not a once-a-year form filer.

- [ ] Confirmed you will work directly with the CPA
- [ ] Discussed the full range of services you need
- [ ] Asked about communication style and off-season availability
- [ ] Felt comfortable asking questions — the relationship matters

---

## Questions to Ask in a First Consultation

A good CPA will welcome these questions. If someone seems annoyed by them, that's a red flag.

1. How long have you been practicing in Rome or NW Georgia?
2. What types of clients do you work with most often?
3. Are you licensed in Alabama as well as Georgia?
4. Who will handle my account day-to-day?
5. What does your process look like from onboarding through tax filing?
6. How do you charge — hourly, flat fee, or a combination?
7. What do you need from me to get started?
