Running a small business in northwest Georgia — or just across the state line in northeast Alabama — means juggling a lot. Payroll, customers, vendors, inventory. Tax deadlines can easily slip through the cracks, and the IRS and Georgia Department of Revenue are not sympathetic when they do.

This post walks through the major federal and state filing dates you should have on your radar for 2026, organized by entity type. Because exact dates sometimes shift when a deadline falls on a weekend or federal holiday, I've flagged every specific date with a reminder to verify it before you act. Confirm current-year deadlines directly with your CPA or at [IRS.gov](https://www.irs.gov) and the [Georgia Department of Revenue](https://dor.georgia.gov).

---

## Why Deadlines Vary by Entity Type

The IRS doesn't treat all businesses the same. Whether you file as a sole proprietor, a partnership, an S-corporation, or a C-corporation changes *when* your returns are due and what forms you file. Getting this wrong — even by one day — can trigger penalties and interest.

---

## Key Federal Filing Deadlines (General Rules)

### Sole Proprietors and Single-Member LLCs (Schedule C)

If your business income flows through your personal return (Form 1040), you're generally on the same cycle as individual filers.

- **Individual return (Form 1040) — generally April 15**- A six-month extension is available, but it extends the *filing* deadline, not the *payment* deadline. If you owe tax, it's still due in April.

> Always confirm the exact 2026 date with your CPA or at IRS.gov — the deadline shifts when April 15 falls on a weekend or recognized holiday.
---

### S-Corporations (Form 1120-S) and Partnerships (Form 1065)

Pass-through entities file earlier than individual returns. The general rule:

- **S-corp and partnership returns — generally March 15**- A six-month extension moves the deadline to **generally September 15**
Filing on time matters even if the business shows a loss, because partners and shareholders need their Schedule K-1s to complete their own returns. Late K-1s create a domino effect.

---

### C-Corporations (Form 1120)

C-corps generally follow the individual April 15 deadline, with extensions available.

- **C-corp return (Form 1120) — generally April 15**- Extension moves the deadline to **generally October 15**
---

### Multi-Member LLCs

By default, a multi-member LLC is treated as a partnership for federal tax purposes, so the **March 15 partnership deadline generally applies**.  If your LLC has elected S-corp or C-corp status, follow those rules instead.

## Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

If you're self-employed or your business income isn't subject to withholding, you're likely required to make quarterly estimated tax payments. Missing these leads to underpayment penalties, even if you pay the full balance at filing.

The four payment periods and their general due dates:

| Payment Period | General Due Date |
|---|---|
| January 1 – March 31 | Around April 15  |
| April 1 – May 31 | Around June 15  |
| June 1 – August 31 | Around September 15  |
| September 1 – December 31 | Around January 15 of the following year  |

Exact dates shift for weekends and federal holidays — verify each one before you schedule your payments.

---

## W-2s and 1099s — Don't Forget January 31

If you have employees or paid contractors, the information-return deadlines hit before most tax filings.

- **W-2s to employees and to the SSA — generally January 31**- **1099-NEC to contractors and to the IRS — generally January 31**
Penalties for late W-2s and 1099s start at $60 per form and climb quickly. If your books aren't clean heading into January, you can find yourself scrambling.

---

## Georgia State Tax Deadlines

Georgia generally conforms to the federal individual and corporate filing calendar, which means your Georgia state return is typically due on the same date as your federal return. As a practical matter:

- Georgia income tax returns for pass-through entities, individuals, and corporations typically mirror the corresponding federal due dates.
- Georgia also offers extensions that parallel the federal extension rules.

For the authoritative source on current Georgia filing deadlines, visit the [Georgia Department of Revenue](https://dor.georgia.gov) or call their taxpayer services line. Do not rely solely on prior-year dates — Georgia occasionally adjusts its calendar.

## Building a Planning Calendar Mindset

The businesses that handle tax season best are the ones that treat it as a year-round process rather than a once-a-year scramble. A few habits that make a real difference:

1. **Mark every deadline in your calendar now.** Set reminders 30 days out and again 7 days out.
2. **Reconcile your books monthly.** You cannot estimate your taxes accurately — or file on time — if your books are three months behind.
3. **Set aside estimated taxes as you earn.** A dedicated savings account for tax reserves prevents the surprise balance-due shock in April.
4. **Communicate with your CPA early.** The March 15 deadline for S-corps and partnerships is tight. If you wait until March to hand over your documents, you're likely looking at an extension — which delays your own personal return.
5. **Verify dates every year.** I've said it several times in this post because it matters: deadlines shift. What was true in 2025 may be one day different in 2026. Check with your CPA or the IRS/Georgia DOR before you act on any specific date.
